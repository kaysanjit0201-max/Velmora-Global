General Repatriation Questions

Deceased remains repatriation is the process of transporting the body or cremated remains of a person who has passed away back to their home country or home city for burial, cremation, or final rites. This requires managing multi-jurisdictional rules, airline security clearances, and health clearances.

Yes. Human remains are shipped as pressurized, climate-controlled cargo in the lower deck compartments of commercial passenger flights. Airlines have strict specifications: remains must be embalmed, secured in a hermetically sealed metal coffin, and enclosed in a sturdy outer wooden shipping crate (coffin box) to comply with international health standards.

Documentation & Clearances

The standard list includes: the Official Death Certificate (cause of death must be non-infectious), the Embalming Certificate, a Coffin Sealing Certificate, a No Objection Certificate (NOC) from the local Indian Consulate in the country of passing, the original passport of the deceased for cancellation, and a local Police clearance report.

The **eCARe** (e-Clearance for Afterlife Remains) portal is the official gateway managed by the Government of India. All inbound remains must be registered on this portal before departure. It submits document files directly to the destination Airport Health Officer (APHO) for pre-approval, helping avoid delays on arrival. Velmora Global handles this portal registration entirely on behalf of the family.

Tariffs, Timelines & Costs

Repatriation costs depend on: local mortuary handling, embalming fees, consular visa processing fees, crate fabrication, ground shipping distances, and airline cargo weight tariffs. Cargo is billed based on volumetric weight (casket + outer crate weight). Our coordinators provide transparent, broken-down quotes before case registration.

For Middle East flights, it averages **2 to 4 days**. From Europe and North America, expect **3 to 7 days**. Timelines depend heavily on municipal office processing speeds for local death certificates, consular office holidays, and cargo flight availability.

Cremated Remains / Urns

Yes, many airlines permit carrying ashes in an urn as cabin baggage, provided you have the Cremation Certificate, Urn Sealing Certificate, and Consulate NOC. The urn must pass X-ray security scanning, meaning it should be made of a scan-friendly material like wood, marble, ceramic, or biodegradable polymer (metals like lead or bronze may block scans and be rejected at security).

Yes. Even when carried as cabin baggage, the ashes must be declared to customs and APHO authorities at arrival. All documents must be inspected to ensure the urn contains only cremation ash and complies with international border controls.

Aviation Logistics

International Packaging Regulations

Before boarding remains on commercial cargo planes, airlines enforce rigid sanitization and sealing rules.

1. Sanitary Embalming

Embalming must be executed by a certified professional undertaker to preserve remains under cargo temperature fluctuations.

2. Hermetic Sealing

Zinc coffin inner liners must be soldered airtight (hermetically sealed) to meet international port health standards.

3. Outer Crate Boxing

Caskets are shielded inside robust outer wooden transport crates to guarantee structural safety inside cargo holds.
